The design philosophy

A 42-key split has roughly a third of the keys of a normal board, so almost everything beyond the letters lives on a layer — a second meaning for the same physical keys, reached by holding (or tapping) one access key. The whole keymap is organised around a few rules that keep that predictable:

Layer keys are single-purpose

Holding an access key always means “this layer.” Tapping a letter always means text. There is no layer-tap on the normal typing path, so a held thumb never second-guesses a fast keystroke.

Modifiers stay on the home row

Ctrl/Shift/Alt/GUI are home-row mods — hold A S D F / J K L ; for the modifier, tap them for the letter. Your fingers never leave home position to chord a shortcut. (details below.)

The right way in matches how long you stay

Layers you visit for a moment are held (Nav, Number). A layer you dip into for one key is one-shot (Function). A layer you sit in to change settings is toggled (Adjust). The access mechanism encodes the intent.

Either hand should cope alone

The Number layer mirrors the numpad onto both hands, so you can type digits one-handed while the other hand is on the mouse. Symbols sit under the left hand while the right hand drives the cursor.

Editing has dedicated keys

Backspace, Delete, word-delete (Ctrl+Bksp), Enter and Escape are real keys on the base layer, not buried on a layer. The most common edits never cost a layer change.

The board tells you where you are

RGB and the OLED report the live layer, modifiers and Caps Word. Held layers deliberately show no “how to leave” hints — you are only passing through. (below.)

How the layers connect

Everything starts and ends on Base. Each arrow is one physical key and the access keycode that does the move. Three of the four moves spring back automatically — the moment you release the held key (or after the one-shot fires) you are home on Base again. Only Adjust is a place you deliberately go and stay.

Why one key does two jobs. The right-inner thumb is OSL(_FUNCTION) on Base but QK_LLCK (Layer Lock) on every other layer. On Base you want a quick dip into the F-keys; once you have held your way onto Nav or Number, the same thumb pins that layer so you can stop holding. One physical key, the sensible job for wherever you already are.

Base layer

default QWERTY, home-row mods, dedicated editing & layer keys.

The alphas are plain QWERTY. Everything special is on the columns and thumbs the letters don't use:

  • Left pinky column stacks the three “mode” keys: Tab, then Caps Word on the physical Caps Lock spot (types one capitalised word, then turns itself off at the next space), then MO Number.
  • Thumbs read left-to-right as Del · MO Nav · Enter  |  OSL Function · Space · Ctrl+Bksp (word-delete).
  • Right pinky column keeps dedicated Bksp, ' and Esc — fast editing with no modifier juggling.
  • Home row carries the GUI/ALT/SHIFT/CTRL mods (the small green labels). They behave as ordinary letters when tapped.

This is the only layer with combos. Pressing two adjacent keys together fires a symbol or a text macro — see Combos & macros.

Navigation + Symbol layer

hold · MO Hold the left-middle thumb. Right hand steers, left hand types symbols.

Symbols were merged into the navigation layer so one hold covers both. The split is intuitive: the right hand — the one not holding the layer — gets the arrow cluster (← ↓ ↑ →) with Home/PgDn/PgUp/End directly below, so cursor movement is a self-contained block. The left hand carries the symbols: the shifted-number row (! @ # $ % / ^ & * ( )) across the top and every bracket pair ([ ] { } \ |) on the lower rows.

The left home row repeats WIN ALT SHIFT CTRL as plain modifiers, so you can chord shortcuts that involve the symbols/arrows without fighting the home-row-mod timing while a thumb is already down. The left-outer thumb becomes TO Adjust; the right-inner thumb becomes Layer Lock so you can park here hands-free.

Number layer

hold · MO Hold the lower-left pinky. The numpad is mirrored on both hands.

The signature move of this layer: the identical numpad is on both hands. Top row 7 8 9 /, home row 4 5 6 0 =, bottom row 1 2 3 . *, with + and − down the outer column. Because the layer is a hold on the left pinky, your left hand can keep that pinky down and still reach its own digits — or you let the right hand do the typing while the left just holds. Either hand can enter numbers alone, which is the whole point when the other hand is on the mouse.

Thumbs mirror the Nav layer: TO Adjust on the left-outer, Layer Lock on the right-inner to pin it, and Enter kept on the left-inner. The left-middle thumb is transparent, so it still falls through to MO Nav from Base.

Function layer

one-shot · OSL Tap the right-inner thumb, then one key from this layer fires.

F1–F12 are laid out to echo a keyboard's function row: F1–F5 on the left top row, F6–F10 on the right top row, with F11 and F12 tucked onto the right home/bottom pinky. Media transport (Prev / Play / Next, Mute, Vol ±) plus Menu and PrntScr fill out the right hand, and Caps Lock sits on the left home pinky.

Because it is one-shot, you tap the thumb and the very next key comes from here, then you are back on Base — perfect for a single F5 refresh or a volume nudge. The thumb cluster is mostly transparent (you're gone after one key); only Layer Lock is wired up, in case you want to stay for a run of F-keys.

Adjust layer

toggle · TO Reached from Nav / Number. The settings room — you go in and stay.

This is the only layer you switch to rather than hold, because changing RGB or locks takes several keypresses. The left hand is the RGB control block — toggle/mode on the pinky, and hue / sat / val / speed up on the home row, down on the bottom row. Top-left has Boot (flash mode) and EE Clr (reset stored settings). The right hand holds the locks (Num / Scroll Lock), brightness, and the two exits back to QWERTY (TO jumps, DF also makes it the default layer).

Unlike the held layers, Adjust keeps its “way out” keys lit, because you are sitting here and need to see how to leave.

Home-row mods, in one picture

Every home-row key is dual-function: tap for the letter, hold for the modifier. They are arranged symmetrically (GUI→ALT→SHIFT→CTRL inward on the left, mirrored on the right), so the strongest fingers get the most-used mods and a two-hand shortcut like Ctrl+Shift+S is split comfortably across the split.

Combos & macros (Base layer)

Press two keys at once on Base and you get a symbol or a small text macro — no layer needed. They are grouped by where they sit so the chords are easy to remember: math/symbols on the right home row, quote-wraps up by O P, and the whole-line operations walking along the bottom row.

Each link joins the two keys you press together; the badge is what it outputs. Toggle a family to isolate it.

How the board talks back

RGB per-key indicators

Each layer lights its own colour — Base coral, Nav pink, Number goldenrod, Function magenta, Adjust red — on just its key keys. Base lights the home row plus the access keys (each in its destination's colour, so the map is self-documenting). Caps Word / Caps Lock turns the pinky spot white/red.

Held layers (Nav, Number) are dimmed and show no “way out” hints — you're only passing through, so they'd be noise. They're also kept few and dim on purpose: too many lit keys browns out the TRRS-powered right half.

OLED status

The off-hand half shows the emdashii logo. The master half shows live status: the layer name (Base / Nav+Sym / Number / Function / Adjust), the last key pressed, typing speed in WPM, and the current modifier / Caps state — so a held mod or an active Caps Word is always visible.
